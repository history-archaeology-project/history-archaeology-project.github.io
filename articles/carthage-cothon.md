# The Cothon of Carthage: The Ancient World's Hidden Naval Base
*Published: July 2026 | Category: Ancient History & Archaeology*

---

For centuries, **Carthage** was the undisputed superpower of the Mediterranean. Its wealth was legendary, and its navy struck fear into the hearts of the Romans. 

But the crown jewel of Carthaginian military engineering wasn't their ships—it was where they hid them. 

Behind the bustling public merchant docks of the city lay a secret, heavily fortified military harbor known as the **Cothon**. It was a structure so advanced that it functioned as a modern aircraft carrier group does today, acting as a high-tech naval base completely invisible from the open sea.

---

## ⚓ A Circular Fortress of Water

The Cothon was divided into two distinct, connected water basins:
1.  **The Merchant Harbor:** A rectangular outer lagoon where foreign traders docked.
2.  **The Naval Harbor:** A massive, perfectly circular inner basin reserved strictly for the Carthaginian navy.

At the center of this circular harbor sat a monumental artificial island housing the headquarters of the Admiralty. From this central tower, the admiral had a 360-degree view of the entire facility and the open sea.

Surrounding the circle were **220 individual docks** (dry docks) built directly into the concentric outer walls and the central island. Each dock was covered by an elegant tiled roof supported by massive Ionic columns, giving the entire harbor the appearance of a continuous, circular temple of war.

---

## 🔒 Complete Military Secrecy

The genius of the Cothon lay in its security and camouflage:
*   **Invisible from the Outside:** A double wall separated the outer merchant harbor from the inner circular military harbor. No merchant, diplomat, or spy from the outer docks could see what was happening inside the military zone.
*   **The Launch:** When Carthage decided to strike, 220 lethal triremes and quinqueremes could slide out of their dry docks simultaneously, launching a massive, coordinated armada into the Mediterranean before the enemy even realized the fleet had left the harbor.
